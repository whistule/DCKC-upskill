A Practical Guide

UpSkill! A guide for DCKC Members

How members can set up coaching, courses and assessments for the club - how the funding works, how to find providers, and what actually makes a course run.

The idea

The club wants to help members initiate and organise the training and assessments they want - with guidance from the club and investment from both DCKC and Paddle Scotland behind them. This guide is a framework to unlock progression.

DCKC has a great record of supporting people in their learning and personal development. We want to support people to take the courses, training and qualifications to develop as paddlers and people. However, we recognise that official training courses only make up a small piece of someone's learning journey. Finding other people who have a similar objective and forming groups for peer-supported learning within the right environments, or finding people to benefit from your coaching (through microsessions etc.), is actually where most of the learning happens. While this guide offers advice on training and courses, it also tries to encourage opening up as much possibility as we can for forming groups to aid our progression and upskilling.

Secondarily, DCKC wants to make courses financially accessible to everyone, not just those who can afford expensive commercial courses.

How we help you make it happen

The club can't run every course someone might want centrally. What it can do is provide the structure, the funding pathway, and the accumulated practical know-how so that any member with the motivation to organise a course for themselves and others has what they need to make it happen.

Two pathways

Some courses and qualifications work best when members find the course and book on individually. Other courses and assessments work best for everyone when the club can pull together a group of members, so that a course or assessment can be run for club members with the costs shared between them. This guide covers both pathways:

  • If you're looking at a course you can book directly with Paddle Scotland, see the Individual sign-up tab.
  • If you're organising or joining a club-run course, see the Group courses tab.

Why is one pathway better than the other in certain situations?

Some courses greatly benefit from splitting the cost of a provider amongst a group, with the provider's rate negotiated at course level. Typically in these cases a whole group of club members can be upskilled for the same cost as someone attending an equivalent commercial course. This also allows us to tap into Paddle Scotland funding for courses we run ourselves.

Other training and qualifications have options that are already quite heavily subsidised at the course level, and it makes much less difference whether they are organised as a group or booked individually. For these, the simpler route is usually for members to sign up directly.

Finding providers

If there is interest in the club, it is beneficial to find a provider to run the course for the club. Some courses aren't great commercial prospects - understanding why helps you approach providers fairly, and increases the chance of a yes.

It is worth being honest about the commercial reality. If a day rate of £200 or £250 is agreed and the day has to be moved four times due to weather, that becomes a much poorer day rate for a provider who is setting time aside for each of those attempts. Four postponements is not unusual for a weather-sensitive assessment.

This goes some way to explaining why some providers may not be enthusiastic about agreeing to run certain courses or assessments, or why they may prefer to run them commercially rather than at subsidised rates. It isn't obstruction - it's economics.

It is also true that some providers communicate better than others, making for more successful learning experiences. Clear communication before the course - about conditions, plans, expectations, and what candidates should prepare - sets everyone up to get more out of the day. This is part of why the club keeps a short list of providers it has worked with well, rather than booking whoever is cheapest or most available.

What helps when approaching any provider

  • Offer a realistic rate from the outset. Don't anchor at £200 for a course that warrants £350. A fair opening offer saves negotiation and shows you understand the work.
  • Come with a full group already lined up. A provider is far more likely to say yes to "we have six club members ready to book" than "we might be able to find some people".
  • Start with the provider's availability, not yours. Providers are busy and their weekends are at a premium. It's usual to begin by asking what dates they have available, rather than offering a preset window. Any course that could reasonably be run on weeknights - because it makes no difference to participants - should be offered that way if it helps the provider's availability.
  • Handle the guinea pigs. For leader assessments, the club arranging the paddlers who act as the group being led is a real contribution the provider will appreciate.
  • Build ongoing relationships. Providers who have had a good experience with the club once will pick up the phone faster the next time - and may end up on the approved list above.

Looking beyond the approved list

When the approved providers can't cover a particular course or date, Paddle Scotland's provider directory is a sensible first stop for recognised coaching. Word of mouth within the club and across sister clubs tends to be the richest source beyond that - ask whoever has run similar courses recently which providers were good to work with. Always run a new provider past the coaching coordinator before committing.

Club policy Any course that is run and subsidised by Paddle Scotland at the course level can be pursued individually, after checking if any other club members fancy joining you. The club is supportive of this route and happy to point members at the right resources, but does not organise or facilitate these courses as group events.

Prerequisites matter

Some qualifications have prerequisites - logbook hours, prior awards, minimum age, or specific prior training (e.g. many coaching courses have Core Coach as a prerequisite). Check these before booking. Paddle Scotland's course pages spell them out, but if in doubt the coaching coordinator can help you work out whether you're ready.

Word of mouth and provider newsletters

Beyond the Paddle Scotland calendar, individual providers sometimes run open courses directly. Mailing lists from providers you've worked with before, or that the club has worked with (see the Group courses → Finding providers tab), are a good source. Other club members who've recently done a qualification are another - ask around.

Booking and paying

For open Paddle Scotland courses, the sign-up process is direct with Paddle Scotland or the delivering provider. The member pays up front at the point of booking. From the club's side there's nothing to organise - though see the next tab for what the club may be able to contribute.

What the club can contribute

Even when a course is organised centrally by Paddle Scotland, the club may offer practical or financial support - particularly for qualifications that feed back into DCKC as coaches and leaders.

Support varies by qualification and by the year's budget, but the principle is simple: when a member's qualification will benefit the club (as a volunteer coach, a trip leader, or someone who can mentor newer paddlers), the club has an interest in helping them get there.

If you've asked around the club and find that the club isn't running a course in your area of interest, and that you're the only one interested in a particular course or progression, and you can't find a Paddle Scotland supported course either, the club will still support you - particularly if you plan to contribute back to the club or already have been doing so. In this case you might actually be adding new expertise to the club.

Important Contributions from the club for individually-booked courses are not automatic. They depend on the qualification, the member's commitment to giving back to the club, and what budget is available. Discuss it with the coaching coordinator before you book, not after.

What might be available

  • Partial reimbursement of course fees for qualifications that directly benefit the club - particularly coaching qualifications for members who will then coach within the club.
  • Kit loan from the club fleet if a specific qualification needs equipment the member doesn't have.
  • Logbook and mentoring support from existing qualified members - sometimes what someone needs isn't money, but someone to paddle with while they build up the required experience.

How to ask

Drop a note to the coaching coordinator with:

  • The course you're looking at (link to the Paddle Scotland page is ideal)
  • Dates and venue
  • Cost
  • Why you want to do it and how you'd expect to contribute back to the club

A short, concrete message is better than a long one. Expect a conversation rather than a form-filling exercise.

Giving back

The club is happy to invest in members' qualifications on the understanding that qualified members contribute back - by coaching, leading trips, mentoring, helping organise club sessions, or being part of the group that runs future courses. This isn't a formal contract; it's simply how the club's coaching and leadership depth grows over time. If you've been helped to qualify, think about what you'd like to offer in return.

Group courses

This is where the club pulls together members into a shared course or assessment, with costs shared between participants and topped up by the club and Paddle Scotland.

For group courses, participants typically contribute £50 per course. Training courses take a full group (ratios vary with the course, but filling to the maximum is always preferred) and assessments typically take two candidates per day. Training courses typically run over two days; assessments are typically a single day.

The club gets support from Paddle Scotland of £100 per day towards a nominal £200 day rate for recognised coaching and assessment. That subsidy, combined with member contributions and the club's own investment, is what makes the economics work. In practice, £200 per day is often not a sufficient rate for many courses or assessments, so the club tops this up - particularly for assessments, which it treats as an investment in its future leaders.

Steps for a successful learning event
  1. Find interested members - typically 5 or 6. Every opportunity for learning, including for guinea pigs, should be sought from and offered to club members through open communication (WhatsApp groups, the club calendar) before looking more widely if required.
  2. Find a provider. See the Finding providers tab for the list of DCKC approved providers and guidance on approaching them.
  3. Find suitable dates. For weather-dependent assessments, multiple dates work better than a single date.
  4. Confirm people's bookings with their payment. The £50 contribution is the booking - no payment, no place.
  5. Circulate info for the course. Use the relevant course info page on the website.
  6. Keep tabs on communications. Make sure everyone knows what's going on - candidates, students, the provider, the committee.

The sub-tabs that follow work through each of these steps in detail - funding mechanics, how to approach providers, the full organising checklist, how sign-up and payment work, communicating with the group, and the course info pages for candidates, trainees, and guinea pigs.

How the funding works

Three parties contribute: Paddle Scotland, the club, and the members taking part. It's easiest to understand the mechanism first, and then see how it plays out across different courses.

The basic mechanism

For each day of a course, Paddle Scotland contributes £100 (via their subsidy) and the club pays a matching £100 to Paddle Scotland. Paddle Scotland then pays the provider £200 for that day. If the agreed day rate is higher than £200, the club pays the difference directly to the provider as a top-up.

Members each contribute £50 per course, regardless of whether it is a one-day assessment or a two-day training course. This flat contribution is what members pay; the club carries the rest.

Training and assessment work differently Training courses take a group of participants - ratios vary with the course and provider, but filling courses to their maximum is always preferred, since this makes the most of the club's and Paddle Scotland's investment. Assessments are separate events, typically take only two candidates per day (this is set by the awarding body, not by the club), and represent an investment by the club into its developing leaders. The club meets the gap between the member contribution and the actual day rate as a deliberate commitment to helping members qualify and give back to the club in turn.
Important Always agree the total day rate with the provider up front. The Paddle Scotland / club-top-up split is an administrative matter; the provider will get payment from two places - Paddle Scotland (£200/day) and a top-up from DCKC.

What day rate applies?

The rate depends on two things: whether the course is weather-sensitive enough to be prone to postponement, and whether the provider typically needs to travel to find conditions. Additional accommodation costs may be necessary if travel is involved. Note that training courses typically run over two days, while assessments are typically a single day.

Getting the group ready for the possibility of travel and postponement - and managing expectations around it - is the other side of the coin. See the Communicating with the group tab.

Assessments are a more significant investment of £100-£150 per day. Why the £200 baseline often isn't enough

The Paddle Scotland £200/day baseline is a starting point, not a ceiling. Rates need to reflect the realities of the particular course - risk of postponement, travel to find conditions, logistical complexity for the provider, scarcity of assessors, and the commercial reality of the provider setting time aside.

An SKL assessment is the clearest illustration. The assessor may need to travel to find suitable water, postpone because the conditions on the agreed day aren't right, and coordinate guinea pigs in advance. That hidden logistical load is work that doesn't show up on the day itself. £200 doesn't cover it honestly; £350 does.

PSL assessment, though local, is similarly weather-sensitive - the Beaufort Force 3 requirement is exacting and means dates are often moved. A £300 rate acknowledges the real risk of postponement eating into the provider's week.

Rule of thumb The more the day depends on weather, travel, or assessors who have to juggle guinea pigs and conditions, the higher the appropriate day rate. Paying properly is what keeps good providers willing to work with the club.

Organising a course, step by step

A rough running order from first idea to day-of. Adjust as needed.

  1. Consider a rough window. Think about the time of year that will give appropriate conditions, position relative to school holidays and club trips, and what the provider is likely to have available. This shapes when you start asking around.
  2. Gauge interest. Post in the relevant club channel to see how many members are interested in a particular course or assessment. Six is a good target; some courses and assessments may only allow fewer. Consider posting in a timely manner relative to the rough window - maintaining momentum between the initial idea and confirmed bookings is valuable.
  3. Approach a provider. Start with the DCKC approved providers on the Finding providers tab - these are people the club has worked with before. Start with their availability (their weekends are at a premium). Offer a realistic day rate (see the rate matrix on the funding page) and the group you have ready. Be upfront about the club's funding setup, and remember the rate needs to be negotiated and agreed with the provider.
  4. Get firmer commitment from participants. Go back to the interested members with the provider's likely availability and a concrete rate. Ask for a firm yes/no. This is where speculative interest turns into a real group.
  5. Notify and confirm details with the coaching coordinator. Before committing to a date with the provider, run the plan past the coaching coordinator - course, provider, date, rate, group size, any top-up required. This catches any conflicts or issues early.
  6. Confirm the date with the provider. A short email is fine. Make sure the total day rate, payment arrangements (from Paddle Scotland and DCKC), and contingency for postponement are all clear in writing.
  7. Open sign-up and collect payment. The £50 contribution is the booking - see the Signing up & payment tab for how to handle this properly. Not collecting payment up front is the single biggest cause of shortfalls.
  8. Notify the club treasurers. So the Paddle Scotland £100 payment and any top-up to the provider can be processed in good time.
  9. Point candidates or trainees to appropriate information. Send them to the relevant page on the Course info pages tab. Flag the possibility of travel and what that may involve (accommodation, fuel, timing) clearly and early - managing expectations is key.
  10. Brief candidates on guinea pigs (leader assessments only). Finding guinea pigs is the candidates' responsibility, not yours - but they need clear guidelines from you up front. See the Communicating with the group tab for what to cover. Do an open call on club channels to start things off, then hand over to the candidates.
  11. Support the day itself. The provider handles the day's logistics - meeting point, launch site, running order. For assessments, these logistics are often handed to the candidates to plan (in collaboration with the assessor), since this is part of what's being assessed. Either way, the facilitator's role on the day is to stay on hand to deal with any issues, rather than to run things. The provider may not know the candidates or trainees at all - a club member who does can add a lot of insight that makes the day go smoothly: who's likely to struggle with what, who's got relevant prior experience, who needs encouragement vs. pushing.
  12. Check in with the provider in advance. Make sure they have everything they need to run the day well - participant list, any relevant info on experience levels or medical considerations, meeting point and timing, venue specifics. A useful prompt: mention the club's standard ICE card arrangements, as providers may not be familiar with how DCKC handles emergency contact info and medical disclosures.
  13. Seek feedback from all parties. Gauge how the day went from the provider, candidates or trainees, guinea pigs, and anyone else involved. Often people will want to tell you anyway - create the space for that. Note where improvements can be made next time.
  14. Close the administrative loop. Make sure the provider is paid promptly, any certification paperwork goes where it needs to, and a short note goes back to the coaching coordinator so lessons carry forward.
A note on postponement Build it into the plan from the start. If the day or course has a heavy chance of postponement - particularly SKL and PSL assessments - it is often wise to agree contingency dates with the provider up front. This allows the payments and fee structure to stay in place and shift to the next suitable date with minimal friction. While it is often standard practice for fees to be waived on a postponed course, it is better to discuss that with the provider in advance of it actually happening, rather than trying to sort it out afterwards.

Who can help

Organisers will be experienced people in their own right, so this isn't a hand-holding exercise. The coaching coordinator, the club treasurers, the providers themselves, and Paddle Scotland are all available to help make an event go well. Most issues that come up can be worked out between people directly - a quick conversation usually sorts things faster than trying to solve it in isolation.

Signing up, payment & cancellation

One hard-won lesson: a verbal commitment is not a booking. Payment is what secures a place.

The commonest mistake when organising a course is taking expressions of interest as if they were confirmed bookings. People mean it at the time, but life intervenes - work trips, injuries, weather nerves - and a few weeks later two of them have quietly dropped out. If no money has changed hands, there's nothing holding the group together. The club ends up carrying the shortfall because the provider still needs to be paid in full.

The rule The £50 contribution is the booking. Until it has been paid, a place is not held. This applies equally to close friends, long-standing members, and the person who organised the course.

How sign-up should work

  1. Open sign-up with a clear deadline. State the course, the date (or date window), the cost, what's included, and when payment is due. Make it obvious that payment secures the place.
  2. Collect payment at point of sign-up. Not "I'll pay you closer to the time". It is usually easier to collect payments to an intermediate account (perhaps your own) and then pass the total to the club account as one lump sum - this lets you keep track of who's paid without constantly bothering the treasurers. Use a clear reference like the member's name and course. Coordinate with the club treasurers on the arrangements for passing funds across.
  3. Confirm the place only once payment has arrived. Until then, it's a provisional interest, not a booking. A waiting list can fill gaps if the course is oversubscribed.
  4. Close sign-up once the course is full. Don't keep taking names informally - it creates ambiguity about who is on and who isn't.

Why this matters

If a six-person course is booked at £350/day, the club has committed £350 to the provider regardless of who turns up. With six paid members, the numbers work. With four paid members and two no-shows who never paid, the club is £100 down and covering the shortfall from general funds. Over a year, that adds up fast - and it quietly discourages the club from running courses at all.

Taking payment up front isn't officious. It's the mechanism that creates buy-in to the event while also making the whole arrangement sustainable, and it protects the organiser from being the person chasing a friend for £50 three weeks after the course.

Cancellations and drop-outs

Even with paid-up sign-ups, people occasionally have to drop out. A fair default policy:

  • If the place can be filled from a waiting list before the course, the dropped-out member gets a refund.
  • If the place cannot be filled, half the contribution (£25) is retained - because the club is still paying the full day rate to the provider.
  • If the course itself is cancelled by the club or provider (not merely postponed), members are refunded in full.
  • If the course is postponed, contributions carry over to the rescheduled date. Members who cannot make the new date should be offered a refund if their place can be filled from the waiting list.

State the policy in the sign-up message so everyone knows the terms up front. It prevents awkward conversations later.

A useful habit Keep a simple spreadsheet or shared document for each course listing name, contact, payment status (paid / pending / refunded), and any relevant notes. It makes handing things over to the committee trivial, and removes the ambiguity that causes the shortfall problem in the first place.

Communicating with the group

A course runs smoothly when the group knows what's going on - before, during, and after. Good communication is what keeps people willing and happy when the weather intervenes. Make it your responsibility to get people the right information. Keep messages concise and limited to what's actually required - and put important information first, or put it in a message of its own.

The funding page deals with the commercial side of a course. This page deals with the other side: getting a group of people ready, keeping them informed, and managing expectations through the inevitable bumps. All parties - particularly those progressing their skills and expertise, which is everyone on the water - will greatly appreciate a course organiser who facilitates the experience well.

Set expectations at sign-up

The single most useful thing you can do is be realistic up front. Most friction on courses comes from a mismatch between what members expected and what actually happens. When opening sign-up, cover these points explicitly:

  • The course may need to travel. For SKL training and assessment, and sometimes for other courses, the group may have to drive to find appropriate conditions. Flag this as a possibility, not a certainty. Candidates and guinea pigs may go to an area they have not visited before - knowing the area in advance is not part of the assessment. In fact, candidates working proficiently in an area they don't know well tends to work in their favour, since it demonstrates exactly the planning, observation, and judgement skills the assessment is looking for.
  • Accommodation may be required. If travel is involved, there may be overnight accommodation to arrange and pay for - separate from the £50 course contribution. Give members an honest ballpark so they can decide before they commit.
  • The course may be postponed. Assessments in particular are weather-dependent. Dates are provisional until the forecast firms up. Members need to be ready for a move.
  • What the £50 covers and what it doesn't. It's the course booking. It doesn't cover personal kit, food, accommodation, fuel, or Paddle Scotland registration.
  • What prior experience is needed. Especially for training courses with a remit - a sentence or two on what level of paddler the course is aimed at saves disappointing conversations later. For assessments, candidates will often be asked to make their own judgement about their readiness; flag this early, as self-assessment is part of what leader qualifications expect.
Travel & accommodation Making the group ready for the possibility of travelling - and managing expectations around it - is the key thing that separates courses that run smoothly from courses that get bogged down in logistics arguments. Flag it at sign-up. Confirm it when dates are fixed. Remind people the week before.

Between sign-up and the course

A quiet group is often an anxious group. A short message every week or so - even just "no news, still on for the 14th" - keeps people engaged and reassured. Things worth sending proactively:

  • Confirmation that the course is going ahead once the group is full and the provider is booked.
  • Link to the relevant course info page (see the Course info pages tab) so candidates and guinea pigs can read the detail at their own pace.
  • Any logistics updates - meeting point changes, travel requirements emerging, kit clarifications.
  • A quiet check on nerves. Try to sense if anyone seems - or is likely to be - nervous about the day, and check in with them individually to see if there's anything you can help with. Often a short reassuring conversation is all that's needed.
  • A check on readiness a week or two before - especially for guinea pigs who may need a reminder about what's expected of them.
  • A check-in with the provider to confirm they have everything they need: participant list, experience levels, relevant medical info, meeting point, timing. Mention the club's ICE card arrangements if the provider isn't already familiar with how DCKC handles emergency contact and medical info.
  • Forecast and go/no-go decision in the final few days for weather-dependent courses.
  • Coordinate the weather call with the provider and candidates. The provider leads the decision, but in some cases they may need a second opinion from someone who knows the group better than they do - how experienced people are, how comfortable they'll be in the conditions being forecast, whether an alternative venue would work for this particular mix of paddlers.
Don't assume the provider has said it A good provider will communicate most of this directly to the group - but it's often worth reiterating information from the organiser side too. It is easy for miscommunications to occur when information is flowing through two channels, and club policies sometimes differ slightly from a provider's standard practice (ICE cards, the £50 booking model, how DCKC handles postponements). Cover the important points yourself and don't assume they've been covered.

Guinea pigs for leader assessments

For PSL and SKL assessments, the candidates need a group of paddlers to lead - the "guinea pigs" (or students). Finding and communicating with these paddlers is the candidates' responsibility, not the course organiser's. But the course organiser should lay out clear guidelines up front, before candidates start reaching out, so everyone operates the same way.

A good provider will also generally lay out guidelines for candidates on finding guinea pigs, but there are additional club policies to add at this point that fall to the organiser:

  • Where possible, guinea pigs should come from within the club. Only look further afield if the spaces cannot be filled by members.
  • There must be an open appeal to club members through the relevant channels - WhatsApp groups and the club calendar - in the spirit of openness and inclusivity to members new and old.
  • We are looking for firm commitments, not half-agreements. Communicate clear expectations to guinea pigs from the outset: what's being asked of them, whether the day may involve travel, and when they need to give a definite yes or no.
  • Firm commitments must be registered with the candidate, the provider, and the organiser - not kept under someone's hat. Everyone needs to know who is on the list before the day.
  • Set up a clear communications channel with the guinea pigs - a group chat that includes the candidate, the organiser, and the provider, so that updates on weather, venue changes, meeting points and timings can be communicated easily to everyone at once.
Why this matters A leader assessment can fall apart if guinea pigs drop out, arrive underprepared, or refuse to travel on the day. Setting expectations up front - at the point of asking, not the morning of - is what keeps this from becoming a crisis. Candidates who handle guinea pig communications well also find the assessment easier: they've already practised the kind of group communication the assessor is looking for.

Handling postponement

Postponement is frustrating but unavoidable for some courses. How it's communicated matters as much as the decision itself. A good approach:

  • Make the decision as early as the forecast allows. People value having time to rearrange their plans more than they value clinging to the original date.
  • Communicate it once, clearly, to everyone at the same time. A WhatsApp group is usually the right channel - avoid one-to-one messages that create an asymmetric information problem.
  • Offer alternative dates immediately if you have them. A postponement with a replacement date feels constructive; a postponement without one feels like a cancellation.
  • Acknowledge the disruption. A short "sorry about this, the provider and I have looked at the forecast and decided..." goes a long way compared to a bare logistical message.

On the day itself

By the time you get to the day, most of the communication work is done. The provider runs the day. For assessments, much of the day's organisation is handed to the candidates - that's part of what's being assessed. The facilitator's role shifts to support: being available if anything needs sorting out, and filling in the gaps between the provider and the group.

  • Confirm the meeting point and time the evening before. Even if it hasn't changed. A single message to the group removes any ambiguity.
  • Be reachable. The provider should have your number for morning-of changes. The group should know who to call if they're running late.
  • Be on hand but don't take over. Show up, help with introductions, and let the provider lead. Offer insight on the participants where it helps (who's got relevant experience, who might be nervous) - this is where a club member who knows the group adds real value.
  • Make sure candidates know what's expected of them in terms of briefing students, handing over, and handing back. The PSL candidate info page covers this in detail.

After the course

A short message after the event closes the loop and makes the next course easier to organise:

  • Thank the provider and confirm payment arrangements.
  • Thank the guinea pigs - their time and willingness made the assessment possible.
  • Share any follow-up - certification timelines, registration requirements, links to further courses.
  • Feed back to the coaching coordinator with a brief note on how the course went. This is what keeps the DCKC approved providers list useful for the next person organising.
The underlying principle Nobody minds a change of plan that's communicated well. Most grumbles on courses are about not being told - not about the actual thing that happened. Err on the side of over-communicating.
